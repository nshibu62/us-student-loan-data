Is a College Loan Right for You?

Some in-depth takes on the pros and cons of using student loans !

College is a great segway to finding out ones career, and their passion moving forward. However, college can be pricy and therefore discourage many applicants from applying and futhuring their educational growth and self-sustaining life. Loans offer an alternative solution to a lack of funds for paying college as students are able to pay their college tuition at a later date.

What is Interest?

In its most simple form, interest is the monetary amount charged on the borrower for the use of a loan. It is essentially an "extra step" taken to your payment before your money can apply to the principal, or the amount/balance of your issued student loan.

How is interest applied?

Interest (unlike your loan payment) is not a uniform amount. It depends on the type of loan you choose, and you personal interest depends on your specific loan amount. Interest may, in some cases, also depends on a concept called subsidization.

What is Subsidization?

Subsidization is an interest-free way during school to utilize student loans and pay them. Subsidized loans means that you are only paying the direct amount of your loan, and the lender takes care of the interest, making it easier on the borrower. The most common type of subsidized loan for students is a

Federally Subsidized Direct Loan.

Find out if you're eligible here. https://studentaid.gov/understand-aid/eligibility

Who is Applicable for a Subsidized Loan?

In order to be eligible for a subsidized loan, you must have these prerequisites:

  • Must be an undergraduate student
  • Be eligible for financial aid
  • Must be obtaining a degree through a specific program
  • Must be attending college for at least half the time.

Standard Repayment Plan
Under this plan, it will take 10 years (or 120 months) to fully complete your loan payment, each pay period being a fixed amount.
Graduated Repayment Plan

Under this plan, loan pay periods are not fixed and increase every two years. The loan completion varies between cases.

Extended Repayment Plan
Under this plan, you can pay your loan for up to 25 years, with generally lower monthly loan pay periods. The pay periods may be fixed or flexible.
Co-Signer
Another person who agree to pay the loan with you, the borrower. Depending on the credit health and records of the co-signer, interest may fluctuate, but co-signers equally affect the loan payment, including penalties and missed payments.
Grants
Not a loan, but a given financial aid that doesn't need to be paid back.

Two Common Types of Loans

Federal

    • Credit check not typically needed
    • More deferment opportunities (student loan postponing based on personal circumstance)
    • Interest rates are usually fixed, lower, and in some cases paid for through subsidization
    • Loan forgiveness is applicable

Private

    • Credit check typically is needed
    • Undergraduate student loans are not subsidized
    • Certain loans give payment penalties for over-extended loan payment times
    • Loan forgiveness is not available
